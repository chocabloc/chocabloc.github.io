# Empirics

This is not a moral argument -- that would require yet another essay. I have tried to appeal to morality as little as possible, but where I have, it is left without justification. 

## 'Neoliberalism'

I should start by noting that nobody has ever been able to produce evidence of an economist arguing for neoliberalism using the words 'trickle-down economics'. The term has been used solely by detractors to misrepresent. In fact, the word 'neoliberalism' itself is usually not used by its proponents today; they prefer 'free-market', 'classical liberal', 'libertarian', etc.

This already hints at what they actually believe in, but here is a short description:

- Individuals produce wealth and respond to incentives.
- WRT negative incentives, state coercion is usually morally bad.
- WRT positive incentives, the state cannot have perfect information of everybody's wants/needs (in particular, it does worse than markets), nor does it have infinitely deep coffers.
- Therefore, free markets are the best way to organise an economy for wealth production.

I think these are true, barring some edge cases and semantic disagreements (for example, there are some reasonable restrictions on individual wants, or production regulations).

## "What about market failures? Pollution and other externalities?"

They exist, but the responses which are agreed on are typically price mechanisms. That is, once externalities come to light, free market reasoning handles them fine (and economists agree on this: e.g. Pigouvian carbon taxes).

Not to mention that you have to compare to real governments rather than idealised governments, and real governments are typically bad at this sort of command-and-control.

## "Okay, wealth production, sure. But what about wealth distribution?"

There are many ways to (re)distribute wealth -- price controls, taxes, nationalisation -- all of which require distorting incentives for production. But maybe that is alright? Maybe we should be fine with less wealth as long as it is distributed better?

Maybe. But one needs to decide what the floor should be. The obvious floor is that everybody should get decent food and shelter. I personally think so, but people differ here, and this obviously changes with time (internet? ai?).

What is true is that (a) different baseline standards require you to assert different amounts of control, and (b) different methods produce different distortions. (a) is obvious.

For an example of (b), price controls are unequivocally bad. You impose a price control on one sector -> they aren't able to remain afloat -> you impose price controls on the inputs, and so on, until at some point you are trying to assert control over basic reality (and are wholly unprepared to do so), and impoverishment follows.

What about taxes? Even though taxes also cause distortions by disincentivising production (for example, 100% taxation produces nothing), you can't do without taxes -- the state requires money to function. I think here one should want the taxes to be as distortion-free and as justified as possible (interestingly the two correlate very well). A general rule of thumb is that you want to tax rent-seeking and not productivity, and you want to tax as little as needed.

## "Nice ideas, but where is the data for all this?"

The data is everywhere. Compare North and South Korea, East and West Germany. Look at China's prosperity following the 1978 reforms, India's GDP growth and consumer access to goods after 1991. Effects of San Francisco rent control and Nixon's wage and price controls. Also what happened to Venezuela, and how well economic freedom indices correlate with prosperity. In fact, the absolute income of the _poorest_ decile is far higher in the freest countries.

### "This is not because of liberalisation, but because of an evil entity which is against socialism."

Two points: (a) nobody is owed foreign trade, and (b) believe it or not, economists do factor in this stuff.

The consensus for Venezuela is that poor policy caused the decline before there were any sanctions. The Cuban embargo is not a global blockade, and Cuba trades freely with Europe, Canada, Latin America, and China. In fact, Cuba was propped up by Soviet subsidies and collapsed precisely when those subsidies ended in 1991.

## "Did you pull all this out of your ass?"

My points are somewhat digested and paraphrased, but you can read _"Trickle Down" Theory and "Tax Cuts for the Rich"_ by Sowell, _The Use of Knowledge in Society_ (and maybe _The Road to Serfdom_) by Hayek, and _Free to Choose_ by Milton Friedman.

## 'Real Economists'

I have no idea why people believe that 'real economists' are Marxists, but this is not true. The majority of economists are against nationalisation and protectionism. The overwhelming consensus is that rent control (and price controls in general) is a bad idea. The opinions are mixed on minimum wage and tax policy, but notice the word 'mixed'.

In the USA, economists are the most politically mixed discipline in the social sciences.

## "Nuh-uh. Everything you said is propaganda and class interests."

I really don't see how I can help you then -- this sort of claim is unfalsifiable.
